WHY SWITCH TO AI / CAREER DIRECTION
====================================================
Honestly? The question I ask myself is: why WOULDN'T I switch to AI?

AI is the future of software — that's not a hot take, that's just reality. Engineers who
don't develop a technical understanding of AI and the ability to work alongside it risk
finding themselves on the wrong side of a very big shift. I've heard a lot of my peers
express anxiety about AI taking their jobs, and I get it. But as someone who has already
successfully made one wildly unlikely career switch, I actually find this moment exciting.

There's so much to learn. And agentic AI — systems that don't just respond but reason,
plan, and take action — opens up a completely new class of capabilities. I don't want to
just use AI. I want to help build and orchestrate it. That's where I'm headed, and the
journey has already started.

I'm currently learning Microsoft's Semantic Kernel framework and working with Anthropic's
Claude Code, and I'm genuinely enjoying every bit of it.

====================================================
THE SLP → SOFTWARE STORY
====================================================
Right before COVID, Michelle started researching a possible move into tech. Her husband was
working as a software engineer at the time, and she'd been watching the field from the
sidelines. She loved working with kids as an SLP — genuinely loved it — but she could see
a ceiling forming. There felt like a natural cap on where the career could go, and after
some honest self-reflection, she started to wonder if it was really the right long-term fit
for her personality. That's not an easy thing to admit after earning a master's degree in
the field.

But the timing turned out to be exactly right. COVID reshaped a lot of things, and Michelle
made the leap. She enrolled in the online Master's in Computer Science program at the
University of Illinois, started building skills, and began interviewing for engineering
roles. She landed at UTIMCO, and describes herself as "incredibly blessed" that they were
willing to take a chance on someone with such limited technical experience at the time.

She hasn't looked back. And here's the part she loves to share: the skills she built as
an SLP — reading people, communicating across different personalities, translating complex
ideas into accessible language — have turned out to be some of her most valuable assets as
a software engineer. She can work with the most technical architects and the least technical
stakeholders in the same afternoon. That's rare, and she knows it.

The real punchline? She went from helping people find their voice to building software —
and now she's building AI that talks back.
